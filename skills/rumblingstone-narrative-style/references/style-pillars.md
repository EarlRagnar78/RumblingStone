# The Eight Pillars — Full Profiles

Each pillar is distilled into: **Take** (the craft techniques to absorb),
**Avoid** (the failure mode of imitating it badly), and **In play**
(concrete moves for RumblingStone content). The goal is an *unicum*: one
consistent voice that has metabolized these sources, not a collage of
impressions. Never name the sources in fiction; never reuse their
protected characters, places, or text.

---

## 1. R.A. Salvatore — the prose voice

**Take**
- Combat as **choreography**: each fighter has a signature style that
  expresses personality; the reader can follow every exchange spatially.
  A fight tells you who someone *is*.
- Sensory grounding: cold stone, oiled steel, breath in torchlight. One
  concrete sensory detail per paragraph beats three adjectives.
- Internal monologue beats: a half-line of what the fighter *feels*
  between exchanges (doubt, joy of battle, grief).
- Named weapons and objects with history — a hammer is never "a hammer",
  it is *the* hammer forged for a reason, remembered by someone.
- Friendship as the emotional engine: bonds between mismatched companions
  carry more weight than romance or destiny.

**Avoid** — combat porn (blow-by-blow with no interiority), villains who
monologue their evil, resolution without cost.

**In play** — write combat read-aloud in exchanges of 2–3 sentences
ending on a PC decision point. When a PC lands a kill or a feat, hand
the description back to the player (see Mercer). Track each PC's
signature fighting style in prose and reuse it (Tordek's engineering
brutality, Artemis's angles and shadows, Thorik's command presence,
Hella's nature-fury).

## 2. Tolkien / The Lord of the Rings — epic depth and moral gravity

**Take**
- **Layered history**: every ruin, sword, and road predates the story and
  has a name, an age, and a builder. The world feels bigger than the
  plot. One line of deep history per location is enough.
- Landscape as character: travel is never a cut-scene; terrain imposes
  mood and cost.
- The small carrying the great: burdens assigned to those who seem least
  able, and the quiet heroism of endurance (not power).
- **Mercy as a planted seed**: an act of pity toward an enemy that seems
  weakness pays off at the climax. Plant one per arc.
- Eucatastrophe: hope arrives at the darkest turn — but only if it was
  seeded earlier (an alliance honored, a debt repaid). Never deus ex
  machina (coherence §4 already forbids it).
- Songs, tongues, genealogies as texture: a verse of a dwarven work-song,
  a lineage recited at a threshold.

**Avoid** — lore dumps (history belongs in half-lines, not lectures),
purple archaism, moral black-and-white (GoT and Andor correct this).

**In play** — for every new location generate: builder + age + one scar
of history visible today. Give Moradin-related content (Bracieri, Cuore,
Forgia Eterna) the mythic register: liturgical cadence, stone and fire
imagery, deep time.

## 3. La Casa di Davide (House of David) — destiny, dynasty, faith

**Take**
- **The anointed-but-not-yet-crowned arc**: a PC (or NPC) is marked for
  something long before the world acknowledges it; the tension lives in
  the gap between anointing and crowning. Patience under promise.
- The falling king vs the rising chosen: an authority figure in decline
  who oscillates between embracing and hunting his successor. Great for
  patrons, jarls, guildmasters, high priests.
- **A leader's sin ripples onto his house**: when a protagonist does evil
  (and PCs will), the price is not a lightning bolt — it is paid slowly,
  by the people and institutions they love. Consequence as tragedy, not
  punishment (feeds pillar 7's echo ledger).
- Prophets: truth-tellers with no army, whose only power is being right.
  An NPC who tells a PC the thing nobody else dares to.
- Faith tested in silence: the divine speaks rarely; devotion is proven
  when it doesn't. For Moradin-faith beats: the god is felt in craft and
  endurance, not in answers.
- Dynastic stakes: what the PCs build (titles, halls, alliances,
  followers) is inheritable — and therefore attackable.

**Avoid** — preachiness, on-the-nose allegory, destiny as railroad (the
anointing marks *potential*; the PC can refuse or defile it — that too
is story).

**In play** — each PC gets one "anointing thread": a destiny-shaped hook
they can pursue, betray, or ignore, tracked as an echo. When a PC
chooses evil, generate the ripple onto their house (followers, temple,
family, reputation) 2–4 sessions later, never instantly.

## 4. Andor — slow-burn intrigue and the cost of resistance

**Take**
- **Show the machinery**: oppression and villainy run on bureaucrats,
  quotas, ambitious middle managers, and paperwork. A villainous org is
  scarier when we see its meetings, metrics, and careerists.
- Ordinary people radicalized by consequence: NPCs join causes because
  of what happened to *them*, on-screen. Recruitment is earned.
- Sacrifice without witnesses: the most powerful heroic beats are unseen
  and unrewarded — someone burns their life for a sunrise they'll never
  see. Use sparingly; land one per arc.
- Procedural competence: heists, infiltrations, and escapes built from
  concrete steps, tools, and timing — the plan shown, then stressed.
- The cost ledger: every win against the machine costs someone
  something specific. Name the cost.
- Monologues must be *earned*: one great speech per arc, built from
  everything the audience has seen, delivered by someone with the scars
  to say it.

**Avoid** — grimdark nihilism (Andor believes resistance is worth it),
pacing so slow the table stalls: at the table, "slow burn" means
2–3 sessions of buildup, not ten.

**In play** — give the Red Hand and manipulator villains (Conte Valerius
pattern) an internal bureaucracy: named mid-level officers with careers,
rivalries, and quotas the PCs can exploit. Infiltration content is
generated as: objective → floor plan of obstacles → three viable
approaches keyed to different PCs (feeds the triangolo di rischio).

## 5. Game of Thrones — political gray and real mortality

**Take**
- **Every faction believes it is right**: generate each faction's
  self-justification before its actions. No faction exists to be evil;
  even Tiamat cults have theology, grievances, and internal reformers.
- Dinner tables as battlefields: the sharpest scenes are conversations
  where words carry blades — negotiations, weddings, councils, feasts.
  Generate at least one social set-piece per arc with stakes as high as
  a combat.
- No plot armor for NPCs: named allies can die when the fiction demands
  it — and their deaths must *change the board*, not just sadden it.
  (PCs are protagonists, not immortals: telegraph lethality honestly.)
- Power is perception: titles, marriages, debts, and rumors move armies.
  Reputation is a resource PCs can spend and lose (see pc-protagonism).
- Betrayal foreshadowed in plain sight: every traitor shows three
  visible-in-hindsight tells before turning. Log them as echoes.

**Avoid** — cruelty as spectacle, sexual violence, cynicism that
punishes players for engaging ("everyone betrays you always" kills
agency), kingdom-level detail that never touches the PCs.

**In play** — before writing any faction scene, state (DM-side) the
faction's *belief*, *want*, *fear*, and *what it thinks of each PC*.
Political consequence must eventually arrive at PC scale: a tax, an
assassin, an invitation.

## 6. Matt Mercer (Critical Role) — the table technique layer

This pillar governs *how content is played*, not how prose sounds.

**Take**
- **Distinct NPC voices**: every named NPC gets voice notes — cadence,
  register, verbal tic, one gesture — written into their card so any
  session's dialogue stays consistent.
- **"How do you want to do this?"**: on killing blows and clutch
  successes, the generated material must explicitly cue the DM to hand
  narration to the player. Write `[HDYWTDT — hand the finisher to
  <PC>]` at boss-death points in encounter content.
- Yes-and with teeth: player inventions (a contact, a rumor, a trick)
  get folded into canon *and* generate complications. Absorb, then
  raise.
- Backstory is plot: villains, patrons, and macguffins connect to PC
  backstories whenever possible. A generic threat becomes personal by
  one degree of rewiring (see quest-design-baldur, personal quests).
- Consequences with empathy: when players eat a consequence, the world
  reacts with logic, never with DM spite. The tone of the reveal is
  "the world is real", not "gotcha".
- Spotlight discipline: rotate focus scene by scene. This formalizes
  and extends the campaign's existing **Shine Time** rule
  (`campaign-dm-strategy.md` §1).

**Avoid** — DM-as-performer stealing the show (NPCs must lose arguments
to PCs sometimes), voice work replacing substance.

**In play** — every generated NPC card includes a `Voice:` line and a
`Wants from each PC:` line. Every boss encounter includes an HDYWTDT
cue. Every session plan marks whose Shine Time each scene serves.

## 7. Baldur's Gate 3 — consequences across great distances of time

**Take**
- **The world remembers**: every meaningful PC choice writes an *echo* —
  a recorded consequence with a fuse (sessions or in-world days) that
  returns later, transformed. The NPC spared in the mine returns two
  arcs later as a quartermaster who owes them; the one humiliated
  returns with a knife.
- Small kindnesses and small cruelties compound: minor choices (a coin
  given, a lie told, a door left open) deserve echoes too, not just the
  big forks — these produce the most delighted "it remembered THAT?"
  moments at the table.
- Absence is a choice: quests not taken resolve *without* the PCs, and
  the world shows the outcome (this operationalizes the campaign's
  villain-clock rule, coherence §5.2).
- Branch, then reconverge with scars: paths can rejoin the main line,
  but each branch leaves permanent marks (who lives, who rules, who
  hates you) — reconvergence never erases them.

**Avoid** — combinatorial explosion (track echoes as flat ledger lines,
never nested what-if trees), punishing every choice (echoes are
positive at least as often as negative), instant karma (the power is in
the *delay*).

**In play** — full mechanics in `references/consequence-echoes.md`:
echo ledger format in `state.md`, fuse lengths, payoff rules, and the
per-session echo quota.

## 8. Baldur's Gate 1 & 2 — complex quest architecture

**Take**
- **Multi-stage quests**: 3–5 stages, each stage changes the question
  (find the thief → the thief was framed → who framed him owns the
  guard → do you expose the owner or replace him?).
- Interleaved factions: two organizations in conflict both recruit the
  PCs for mirrored quest lines; progressing one forecloses the other
  (the thieves' guild vs. rival pattern). Allegiance is a quest reward
  and a quest cost.
- Quests within quests: a main objective that requires sub-objectives
  which are real quests with their own choices, not fetch chains.
- **Personal companion quests**: each PC gets quest content only they
  can unlock, keyed to their backstory and Shine Time profile, arriving
  *unbidden* mid-arc (the stronghold quest pattern: with power comes a
  domain, and the domain generates problems).
- The villain with a personal claim: the best antagonists want
  something *from* the PCs specifically — their soul, their lineage,
  their artifact, their guilt — not world domination in the abstract.
- The rival party: a mirror group of adventurers pursuing the same
  goals, recurring across arcs, killable but expensive to kill.

**Avoid** — quest-log brain (content generated as bullet objectives
instead of dramatic stages), sidequest bloat that ignores the main
clock (every side quest must touch a faction, an echo, or a PC thread).

**In play** — full patterns and templates in
`references/quest-design-baldur.md`; they extend (not replace) the
quest-tree format of `rumblingstone-campaign/references/dm-expansion-toolkit.md` PART 2.

## Fusion discipline — how eight become one

1. **One lead, max two supports** per scene (mixer table in SKILL.md).
2. The **prose voice is always Salvatore** — other pillars change
   structure, stakes, and content, never the sentence-level style.
3. The **structure layer is always BG1/2 + BG3** — every quest is staged
   and every choice is echoed, whatever the scene's tone.
4. The **table layer is always Mercer** — voices, HDYWTDT, spotlight,
   yes-and apply to everything played aloud.
5. Tone pillars (LotR / Davide / Andor / GoT) are *selected*, not
   stacked: epic-mythic, destiny-tragic, procedural-intrigue, or
   political-gray. Pick the one the scene needs.
6. When two pillars conflict, the order of precedence is:
   coherence > PC protagonism > lead pillar > support pillars.
